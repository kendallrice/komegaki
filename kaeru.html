The Ballad of Kaeru and Sasori

Kendall Rice

Sasori-san (a scorpion) was peeking out one day

From underneath the boulder where he whiled his hours away,

And from that crack today looked just like every day before,

And dear Sasori wondered if the world were made of more.

He left his rock that afternoon and scuttled bravely forth

To see just what the west was like, and east, and south, and north.

For many days and many nights he wandered to and fro

Until a river rose ’twixt him and where he wished to go.

Sasori-san could scarcely swim; but ere his hopes were lost,

He spied among the reeds a way by which it might be crossed.

This Kaeru-san (a frog) was kind of heart and strong of leg,

Of which Saso was well aware as he began to beg:

“Good morning, Master Frog! Please help a creature in distress

And ferry me across this stream, for I am flipperless.”

But Kaeru said: “Dear sir, if I should bear you to that side,

How can I know you’ll keep your stinger out of my poor hide?”

To which the scorpion answered: “I shall not, for we’d both die

If I did that—of all God’s creatures none swims worse than I.”

“That may be true,” said Kaeru-san, “but who’s to guarantee

That when we’re close to shore you won’t decide to murder me?”

Sasori-san could only laugh. “My friend, you’ve thought this through.

Supposing I desired to return? Why, I’d need you!”

All this made sense to Kaeru, who posed just one question more:

“But mightn’t you still sting me after we’ve arrived on shore?”

“Kind Master Frog, you do me harm,” Sasori made reply,

“For who on earth could ever will his savior thus to die?”

So logical his arguments, so noble his intent

That Kaeru-san, for all his skepticism, gave consent.

The devious arachnid boarded then his faithful steed,

Who kicked against the current with astounding strength and speed.

Pure-hearted Kaeru-san continued on his watery track

Until, with half the river left to ford, he felt his back

Erupt in agonizing pain from Saso’s poison sting

And croaked: “Foul wight, you’ve doomed us and you haven’t gained a thing!

For surely you must know we both are now condemned to die—

Sasori-san, you bastard bug, give me one reason why!”

The last words gentle Kaeru heard on earth rang warped and shrill:

“It’s just my nature”; then for frog and scorpion all was still.
