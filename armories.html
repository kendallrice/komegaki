Armories and Orchards

Kendall Rice

A certain land which time forgot, and not without a reason,

Supports a pair of moss-encrusted mounds,

On each of which a certain fruit is always found in season:

Atop the one, the apple blooms;

Atop the other, orange perfumes

The balmy air that in that clime abounds.

A town sprang up on either hill, an eye fixed on the other

Across the brook that blued their common green,

Regarding it now friend, now foe, now alien, now brother;

The Orangemen watched their city’s troves

While counting up the apple groves

And often made comparisons between.

For though they must have noticed that their crop was good for eating,

They noticed somewhat quicker that the trees

Were very hard, and rather long—just right, in fact, for beating

One’s foe about the chest and throat

And showering soil upon his coat

And specially for buckling up his knees.

Our kingdoms waxed, their armories from orchards thus converted,

And fashioned all their furniture from stone

Against the day when fructal rights would need to be asserted,

Preferring that their wood be saved

And left intact, with limbs unshaved,

To be applied to hostile flesh and bone.

The clouds had not been stingy with their leaf-refreshing beverage

The year that Pax the peddler paddled by;

The Orangemen, in their constant quest for military leverage

Observing twenty apples more

Than they had spied the year before,

Resolved that every Appleman should die.

Without delay they fetched their saws, their scissors, and their scythes

And razed their gardens for the public good,

Surrendered all their finest crop for military tithes

To outfit every Orangeman’s arm

With plants to do the Apple harm

And teach his skull the sting of citrus wood.

The Apple folk, suspecting something of the clash to come,

Plucked up their barks with equal industry,

Equipped themselves with trunks enough to knock the whole world numb,

And ordered their defensive rank

In rows along the riverbank,

Which rows shocked Pax into soliloquy.

“I say,” said he to no one, for his kayak seated one,

“Methinks the timber-wasters war anew,

If which be true, my business in these hills has scarce begun—

For surely folk who squander thus

Their leaf and bark will make no fuss

Should I relieve them of their silver, too.”

Ere the celestial Orange could slip behind the mossy west,

The swarthy sons of citrus had arrived

Upon the river’s other shore, a bush on every breast—

“On, Orangemen!” rang the opening shout—

The water crossed, their leaves lashed out

In vengeance for their fields, of rain deprived.

Horrendous was the din of bark on bark, and bark on flesh

As bark-struck shrubplates fell from battered ribs;

From blasted stumps to cracking limbs to fruit-sticks green and fresh,

Organic mortar rained and crashed

On human limbs already smashed

Or punctured by the sharper truncheons’ nibs.

Among these scenes the Orangemen’s prince, a lad called Phloem, shoved

In quest of Castle Apple, and the bed

In which he might that evening serve the country that he loved

By joining Princess Xylem there

And shearing from her every hair

Of Xylem’s celebrated maidenhead.

In Xylem’s room another man the princess had received,

But this no Orangeman: this a wandering peddler

By name of Pax, and neutral, if he were to be believed,

Who vowed, for half the country’s treasure,

To grant her from his wares a measure

Whereby she might become the conflict’s settler.

This sounded fair to Xylem, and she presently escorted

The peddler to her father’s silver store,

Of whose supply precisely half he to his purse transported,

Upon which Pax, no liar he,

Bestowed on her the promised key

That in her hands alone could break the war

No sooner was the business sealed, the milky moon ascended,

Than footsteps from the hall spooked Pax away;

The door fell ope, and Xylem gasped, for what walked in was splendid:

A lad becaped, bescarfed, in hose,

Armed not with tree, but rather—rose—

Yet feared she what the lad had come to say.

For his part, Phloem suffered unprecedented remorse

When drank his eyes her beauty sad and pale,

And yet to her “What will you?” quoth he, “Taste your fruit, of course”;

His charging ram would not be lambed—

Her startling eyes, pink lips be damned!—

For Orange he’d ravish her and spread the tale.

Prince Phloem sneered and leveled papery petals at his prey;

The princess Xylem heaved a ponderous sigh;

Anon she drew a sapling with the pot wherein it lay

And crossed his rose; her timor gone,

Took one step forth—the duel was on,

And something savage smoldered in her eye.

Without, the deafening music only trees make when they war;

Within, the silent dance of rival fruits;

One hates the other, hates her, but perhaps he loves her more;

Two swish their brands in earnest strife,

Two living weapons, starved for life;

One thorn rends one white cheek—the crimson suits.

The prince transfixed, he dumbly stood, beholding life’s escape

And praising that reserve which flowed beneath

The gash his token lately carved—where now his taste for rape?—

“I’m won,” she whispered, “nearly so—

If you but take this box and go

Back Orangeward, I my apple shall bequeath.”

So dazed was simple Phloem that he straightaway complied:

He seized the peddler’s box and took his leave,

Fell back among his Orangemen, who prevailed to peer inside—

An insect reared its feelered form—

A hundred more—and then a swarm

The citrus spears and rams began to reave.

Through celluloid these devils drilled as if through clotted cream,

And tasted so, to see them chomp it down;

Prince Phloem pinched, then punched himself in hopes it was a dream—

His rose dissolved in insect jaws,

His shoulders crawled with insect claws,

His torso writhed below a chitinous gown.

Just when our prince had longed to change his flesh for celluloid

So he might be devoured next, and so spared,

Just then, his chief commander sent the swarm into the void

By spraying something from a can

It seems he purchased from a man

Called Pax, with whom half Phloem’s silver shared.

The towns of Apple and of Orange kept up their little war

As long as they had wood enough to swing,

And gave it up abruptly when they found themselves too poor:

For both sides ached from such a cost

As made them claim they both had lost—

But Pax the peddler rowed away a king.

The silver we have won today tomorrow may be spent;

The tale we’ve weathered here we’ll soon forget,

And time forgets us too, and leaves us wondering where it went;

But even if the wind should freeze

Our orchards and our armories,

Be they but separate, there’s hope for us yet.
