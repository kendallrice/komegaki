The Census-Taker

Kendall Rice

A man with a clipboard appeared at our door

To request that we let him inside:

“The guv’ment needs tellin’ how many are dwellin’;

I trust you’ve got no one to hide?”

“We’re four,” said my dad, counting kids on his hand.

Said the stranger, “I’ll see for myself.”

He stomped in right past us (for there we’d amassed us)

And muttered, “Two plates on a shelf.”

The plates thus recorded, “a sofa” and more did

The man with the clipboard proclaim:

“One ottoman, tasteless; this doily a waste is,”

But wrote he them down all the same.

“Excuse me,” said Mom, “all the family is here.

There are four of us. See?” And he cursed,

For she’d made him lose track of the coats on the rack

And he had to re-start from the first.

“A table, four chairs, and a cushion for each”

Was the sum of his work in the kitchen;

My parents, presumin’ he’d come to count humans,

Kept waiting to hear themselves mentioned

Till finally my dad, full of civic concern,

Interjected, “Sir, shouldn’t your notes

Be an enumeration for representation?

I don’t think my ottoman votes!”

“No Ottoman’s voted since 1919—

Or before, ’cept for maybe the sultan.

You shoulda seen his tricks for drawin’ the districts!

What good can such ’manderin’ result in?

Thank God,” said the man, as he tallied our desks,

And their lamps, and their bulbs, “for our system

That tells us all fair our congressional share

By our muebles, si sólo we list ’em!”

Now why he spoke Spanish, I understood less

Than I did this apportioning business,

And why different factions assigned different fractions

Of votes to a chair gave me dizziness.

I feared for the fate of my beanbags, and balked

When I saw how exactly he’d written ’em—

“Boy, don’t be absurd; for Amendment the Third

Says that no one is comin’ to sit in ’em.”

And after he left, my father said, “Kids,

That’s democracy, and you must learn it: your

Legislators you choose; if they don’t share your views,

They’ll at least reflect those of your furniture.”
